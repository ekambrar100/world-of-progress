A World of Progress

The remarkable global decline in infant mortality from 1990 to 2022

A visualization by Ekam Brar for DS 6390

Over the past three decades, the world has witnessed a remarkable transformation in infant survival rates. This visualization explores how countries across the globe have made significant strides in reducing infant mortality, saving millions of young lives.

Scroll down to explore this journey of progress.

1990: A World of Challenges

In 1990, global infant mortality stood at alarming levels. Across the world, approximately 65 out of every 1,000 infants born alive did not survive their first year.

Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia faced particularly severe challenges, with many countries experiencing infant mortality rates above 100 per 1,000 live births.

Healthcare inequality, limited access to vaccines, and poor maternal care were major contributors to this crisis.

The Challenge of Sub-Saharan Africa

In 2000, while some progress had been made globally, Sub-Saharan Africa continued to face significant challenges. The AIDS epidemic, political instability, and limited healthcare infrastructure posed severe obstacles to improving infant survival rates.

Despite these challenges, innovative approaches to healthcare delivery were beginning to take shape across the continent.

India's Journey

India, home to one of the world's largest child populations, made significant progress in improving infant survival. Between 1990 and 2022, India reduced its infant mortality rate by about 70%.

National initiatives focused on maternal health, childhood immunization, and community health workers played a crucial role in this transformation.

China's Rapid Transformation

China demonstrated remarkable progress, reducing infant mortality by over 80% since 1990. By 2010, China's healthcare system reforms had created widespread access to maternal and infant healthcare services.

This transformation shows how policy commitment and healthcare investment can lead to dramatic improvements in child survival.

Rwanda: An Excellent Case Study

Rwanda presents one of the most inspiring success stories. Despite the devastation of the 1994 genocide, Rwanda rebuilt its healthcare system and achieved some of the most dramatic improvements in infant survival globally.

Between 1990 and 2022, Rwanda reduced its infant mortality rate by almost 70%, demonstrating how determination and focused public health initiatives can overcome even the most challenging circumstances.

Latin America's Progress

Countries across Latin America made substantial progress in reducing infant mortality. Brazil, for example, reduced its infant mortality rate by over 75% between 1990 and 2020.

Successful vaccination campaigns, improved sanitation, and wider access to prenatal care contributed significantly to these improvements.

2022: A Changed World

By 2022, the global infant mortality rate had fallen to approximately 27 per 1,000 live births – a reduction of nearly 60% since 1990.

This progress translates to millions of lives saved and represents one of humanity's greatest achievements in recent decades.

However, significant disparities remain between regions and within countries, reminding us that our work is far from complete.

The Path Forward

While celebrating remarkable global progress in reducing infant mortality, we must acknowledge the challenges that remain. In 2022, approximately 3.5 million infants still died in their first year of life – nearly all from preventable or treatable causes.

The data shows that progress is possible in even the most challenging contexts. Looking forward, continued investment in maternal and child health, strengthened healthcare systems, and addressing inequality will be essential to ensure every child has the opportunity to thrive.
